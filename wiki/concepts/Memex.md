# Memex

**Type:** Historical Concept/Prototype
**Proposed by:** Vannevar Bush
**Year:** 1945

## Summary

Memex is a hypothetical information storage and retrieval system described by Vannevar Bush in his 1945 article "As We May Think." It envisioned a personal, curated knowledge store with associative trails — a precursor to modern hypertext and the World Wide Web.

## Key Concepts

**Associative Trails:** Unlike traditional indexing systems that organize information hierarchically, Memex would allow users to create trails through information, connecting related ideas through association rather than rigid categories.

**Personal Knowledge Store:** Each user would have their own Memex device containing their personal library of documents, notes, and connections — tailored to their specific interests and needs.

**Microfilm Storage:** Bush proposed using microfilm to store vast amounts of information in a compact desktop device.

## Historical Significance

**Visionary Design:** Memex anticipated many features of modern digital systems:
- Hypertext linking
- Personal knowledge management
- Associative information retrieval
- User-curated information spaces

**Unsolved Problem:** Bush identified the need for associative information trails but couldn't solve who would maintain the connections and keep the knowledge base organized. The maintenance burden was the unsolved challenge.

## Connection to LLM Wiki Pattern

The [[LLM-Wiki-Pattern]] directly addresses the problem Bush couldn't solve: maintenance. While Memex provided the vision of associative knowledge trails, the LLM Wiki pattern provides the maintenance mechanism — LLMs can update cross-references, maintain consistency, and organize information at near-zero cost.

As [[Andrej-Karpathy]] notes: "The part Bush couldn't solve was who does the maintenance. The LLM handles that."
